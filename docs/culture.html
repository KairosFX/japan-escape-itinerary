Etiquette

How To Move Smoothly

The goal is not to memorize everything. It is to avoid the few behaviors that stand out for the wrong reason.

Temples And Shrines

Respect The Quiet Zones

Speak softly, watch for no-photo signs, remove shoes when asked, and do not stop in the center of narrow approaches.

Trains And Stations

Let The Flow Work

Queue cleanly, keep bags compact, avoid phone calls on commuter trains, and move away from doors once you board.

Street Basics

Do Not Block The Walkway

Before eating, checking directions, or regrouping, step to the side. It matters most in Kyoto lanes, stations, and busy Osaka or Tokyo sidewalks.

General Safety

Stay Calm, Stay Findable

Keep some cash, a charger, and the hotel pin handy. On late nights, care more about the last clean route home than squeezing one extra stop in.

Quick Culture Wins These cover most first-time mistakes.
  • Quiet beats performative enthusiasm.
  • Posted signs beat assumptions.
  • Queue lines matter.
  • Trash bins are limited, so keep a tiny bag.
  • Group photos work better after stepping aside.
Onsen Basics

Keep The Bath Stop Easy

Hakone is where these notes matter most. The short version is wash first, keep devices away, and stay quiet.

Onsen spaces are built around calm shared use. If you are unsure, watch the room for ten seconds and follow the local pace instead of improvising.
Before Entering

Wash First

Clean yourself thoroughly at the shower station before stepping into the bath. This is the core rule.

In The Bath

Keep Towel And Hair Out

Small towels stay out of the water, and long hair should be tied up. Phones stay away from the bath area.

Before Changing

Check Tattoo Policy

Some properties allow tattoos, some still limit them. Ask quietly before you get settled if there is any doubt.

What To Bring

Keep It Small

Bring only the basics: small towel, skin care, hair tie, and a simple change if your stay setup does not already cover it.

Useful Phrases

Short Japanese That Actually Helps

Keep these simple. Clear, polite, and slow works better than overbuilding the sentence.

Excuse Me

Sumimasen

The best all-purpose opener for getting attention, asking for help, or gently moving through a space.

Thank You

Arigato Gozaimasu

Useful everywhere from stores to station staff. Polite and easy to remember.

Directions

... Wa Doko Desu Ka?

Add the place name first, then ask where it is. Example: "Shibuya Sky wa doko desu ka?"

Photos

Shashin O Totte Mo Ii Desu Ka?

Use this when you want to ask if a photo is okay, especially in smaller spaces or near staff.

Help

Tetsudatte Kudasai

A plain way to say "please help me" if you are lost or need support fast.

Bill

Okaikei Onegaishimasu

Use this to ask for the check at restaurants or cafes.

Photo Prompts

One Memory Shot Per City

These are one-line prompts so anyone in the group can walk away with one clean memory frame.

Osaka Stand a little back in Shinsekai after the signs turn on and angle upward for the neon-stack look.
Kyoto Go early or late around the temple-side slopes so the old-street view still has breathing room.
Hakone Use the softer light around Lake Ashi or the shrine approach instead of harsh midday glare.
Mt. Fuji Area If Fuji is visible, go earlier than feels necessary and take the clean mountain shot before experimenting.
Tokyo Capture the street energy first, then do the skyline close once the lights fully come on.
